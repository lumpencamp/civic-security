# Organizational OPSEC: Defending Against Infiltration

Digital security tools are useless if your organization's internal procedures are flawed. The biggest threats to activist groups are often not hackers, but informants, undercover officers, and internal conflicts. This guide outlines strategies for Organizational OPSEC.

## Vetting and Trust

Trust should be earned through action, not assumed through shared ideology.

*   **Vouching System:** New members should ideally be vouched for by an existing, trusted member.
*   **Gradual Integration:** Do not give new members immediate access to sensitive planning chats, financial accounts, or member lists. Start them on low-risk tasks (e.g., flyering, public outreach) and observe their reliability over time.
*   **Beware the "Super Volunteer":** Be cautious of individuals who arrive out of nowhere, immediately volunteer for everything, offer unlimited resources (cars, money, secure spaces), and push for illegal or highly risky actions. This is a common profile for provocateurs or undercover officers.

## Compartmentalization

If an organization operates as one giant group chat where everyone knows everything, one compromised member compromises the entire organization.

*   **The Cell Structure:** Organize into smaller, focused working groups (cells) based on tasks (e.g., media, logistics, direct action).
*   **Need to Know:** Members of one cell should not know the intricate details of another cell's work unless absolutely necessary.
*   **Decentralized Information:** Avoid creating "master lists" of all members or donors. If such a list must exist, it must be heavily encrypted and accessible only to a select few.

## The Canary Trap

If you suspect there is an informant or a leak within your group, you can use a "Canary Trap" (also known as a Barium Meal) to identify them.

1.  **Create Variations:** Take a piece of sensitive information (e.g., a fake meeting location or a fake target for a protest).
2.  **Distribute Differently:** Give a slightly different version of this information to different suspects. (e.g., tell Person A the meeting is at the library, tell Person B it's at the park).
3.  **Monitor the Leak:** Wait to see which version of the information is leaked to the opposition or the police. If the police show up at the park, you know Person B is the leak.

## Security Culture

Security culture is a set of shared customs and behaviors that members of a group follow to minimize risk.

*   **Don't Gossip:** Do not discuss the sensitive actions of others, even if you weren't involved.
*   **Don't Ask Questions:** If someone is doing something sensitive, don't ask them for details unless you have a "need to know."
*   **Consent to Risk:** Ensure everyone involved in an action clearly understands the legal and physical risks beforehand.
