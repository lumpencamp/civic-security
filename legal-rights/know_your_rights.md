# Know Your Rights: Digital Privacy and Field Encounters

*Status: Legal Defense Manual | Audience: Activists, Organizers, and General Public*

Understanding the constitutional framework governing encounters with law enforcement is your first line of defense. This manual provides a granular breakdown of your Fourth and Fifth Amendment rights, specifically focused on the seizure, search, and compelled unlocking of digital devices during field interrogations or arrests.

## 1. The Anatomy of a Police Encounter

Police interactions fall into three legal categories. You must rapidly identify which category you are in to understand your obligations.

### The Consensual Encounter
Law enforcement can approach anyone in public and ask questions. You have zero legal obligation to engage, answer, or produce identification (unless driving).
*   **The Protocol:** Ask, **"Am I free to leave?"** If the answer is yes, walk away calmly. Do not run.

### The Investigative Detention (Terry Stop)
If police have "reasonable suspicion" you are involved in a crime, they can temporarily detain you. You are not free to leave.
*   **The Protocol:** In Illinois, if lawfully detained, you must provide your name and address if asked. You do **not** have to answer any other questions.
*   **The Assertion:** State clearly: **"I am invoking my Fifth Amendment right to remain silent. I will not answer any questions without an attorney present."** Then, remain completely silent.

### The Arrest
If police have "probable cause," you are under arrest.
*   **The Protocol:** Do not physically resist, even if the arrest is unlawful. Physical resistance generates new, severe charges. Repeat the Fifth Amendment assertion above.

## 2. Digital Device Seizures and Searches

The Fourth Amendment protects against unreasonable searches and seizures. A smartphone is not a wallet; it contains the entirety of your digital life. The Supreme Court (*Riley v. California*, 2014) unanimously ruled that police generally may not, without a warrant, search digital information on a cell phone seized from an individual who has been arrested.

### Defending Against Warrantless Searches
Police may attempt to search your device through coercion or by claiming "exigent circumstances."
*   **The Protocol:** Never voluntarily hand over your unlocked phone. If an officer asks to look at your phone, you must verbally and explicitly refuse.
*   **The Assertion:** State clearly: **"I do not consent to a search of this device, its contents, or my physical person."**
*   **Why It Matters:** If police search the device anyway, your explicit verbal refusal preserves your lawyer's ability to file a Motion to Suppress the evidence later in court, arguing the search was illegal.

### Handling Illegal Field Seizures
If law enforcement physically takes your device from your hands or pockets without a warrant:
1.  **Do Not Resist:** Do not attempt to wrestle the device back.
2.  **State Non-Consent:** Repeat: "I do not consent to the seizure or search of that device."
3.  **Lock It Down:** If the device is still in your hand before seizure, use the hardware kill-switch (e.g., holding the power and volume buttons on iOS/Android) to force it into a "Before First Unlock" (BFU) state, disabling biometrics.

## 3. The Compelled Unlocking Dilemma: Passwords vs. Biometrics

The legal landscape diverges sharply based on *how* your phone is locked.

### The Fifth Amendment and Passphrases
The Fifth Amendment protects you from being compelled to be a witness against yourself. This applies to the contents of your mind.
*   **The Law:** Because a passcode, PIN, or swipe pattern exists only in your memory, courts generally hold that forcing you to reveal it is a violation of your Fifth Amendment rights against self-incrimination.
*   **The Protocol:** If presented with a warrant demanding access to the phone, let them take the phone. **Never provide your PIN.** State: "I invoke my Fifth Amendment right against self-incrimination and decline to provide my passcode."

### The Vulnerability of Biometrics (FaceID / TouchID)
Your fingerprint or face is considered a physical characteristic, not "testimony" from your mind.
*   **The Law:** Many courts (though jurisprudence is evolving and varies by jurisdiction) have ruled that police **can** legally compel you to press your finger to a sensor or hold a phone to your face to unlock it, even during a field stop if they have a warrant, or sometimes during an arrest process.
*   **The Protocol:** This is why disabling biometrics prior to entering a high-risk environment is the most critical digital security protocol. If you are detained and your phone relies on FaceID/TouchID, police can physically force the device into your line of sight or press your hand to it. **Always use a complex alphanumeric passphrase.**

## 4. International Border Exceptions

The Fourth Amendment is severely diluted at international borders (including airports). Customs and Border Protection (CBP) claims broad authority to conduct warrantless, "routine" searches of electronic devices.
*   **The Protocol:** CBP can seize your device, copy its contents, and hold it for weeks. If you refuse to provide your password at a border, they cannot legally compel you, but they *will* seize the device and may deny entry if you are a non-citizen.
*   **The Defense:** The only effective defense is to travel with a "clean" burner device, or completely wipe your primary device before crossing, restoring your encrypted data from a secure cloud backup only after you have cleared customs.
